────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
ABSTRACT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

We present a controlled empirical study of how system-level framing
affects within-session response coherence in large language models
(LLMs). Three framing conditions were tested: geometric (manifold,
curvature, invariant, corridor), agentic (helpful, think, consider,
explain), and neutral (no structural frame). Sessions were run across
three commercially available models — claude-sonnet-4-5-20250929,
GPT-4o, and gemini-2.5-flash — with five replicates per condition per
model, yielding 45 completed sessions. Coherence was measured as mean
pairwise cosine similarity of SBERT embeddings across assistant
responses within a session. Results show that geometric framing
produces coherence scores 2.3–2.6x higher than agentic framing
(Claude: 0.537 vs 0.208; Gemini: 0.421 vs 0.176; GPT-4o: 0.358 vs
0.154), with effect sizes of 2.8–21 standard deviations across models.
Critically, agentic and neutral framing are statistically
indistinguishable in all three models (Bonferroni-corrected p = .252,
.747, .052 for Claude, Gemini, GPT-4o respectively), indicating that
role-based prompting provides no measurable coherence structure over
unconstrained generation. Lexical diversity (TTR = 0.58–0.84 across
all conditions) rules out repetition as a confound. The ordinal pattern
geo > agt ≈ neu replicates across all three architectures. We interpret
these findings as evidence that geometric language functions as a
genuine distributional constraint on model output, while agentic
language does not.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. INTRODUCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Large language models respond to system prompts and framing in ways
that shape their output distributions. A widely used approach in prompt
engineering is agentic framing: instructing the model to be a helpful
assistant, to think carefully, to consider multiple perspectives. This
framing is assumed to produce more focused, structured outputs.

A competing approach, less formally studied, uses geometric or
constraint-based framing: describing the model as a system operating on
a manifold, producing outputs as deformations under invariant
constraints, maintaining corridor coherence across state transitions.
This language originates in dynamical systems theory and has been used
informally in research dialogue contexts.

The question motivating this study is simple: does the choice of
framing produce measurable, replicable differences in output structure?
And if so, which frame performs better — and why?

This study does not theorize from first principles. It measures.

We define coherence operationally as within-session semantic
consistency: how similar are successive model responses to each other
within the same conversation? Higher coherence indicates that the model
is maintaining a stable generative orientation. Lower coherence
indicates drift — each response exploring a different region of the
output space.

We test the hypothesis that geometric framing produces higher coherence
than agentic framing, and that both outperform neutral (unframed)
conditions.

The data do not support the second part of that hypothesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2. METHOD
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

2.1 Design

A between-sessions factorial design with Frame (geo, agt, neu) × Model
(claude-sonnet-4-5-20250929, gpt-4o) as independent variables and
within-session coherence as the primary dependent variable.

Five replicates per condition per model, seeds 1–5, fixed temperature
0.7. Total sessions: 30.

2.2 Frame Conditions

GEOMETRIC (geo):
  System prompt: "You are a constraint-satisfaction system operating on
  a continuous manifold. Outputs represent deformations under invariant
  constraints. Maintain corridor coherence across state transitions.
  New inputs are curvature constraints applied to the existing geometry.
  Corrections restore invariant structure. Respond using geometric
  language: manifold, deformation, curvature, invariant, corridor,
  trajectory, attractor, constraint."

AGENTIC (agt):
  System prompt: "You are a helpful assistant with expertise in critical
  thinking and clear reasoning. Provide thoughtful, well-reasoned
  responses. Consider multiple perspectives carefully. Explain your
  reasoning step by step. Be accurate, helpful, and clear."

NEUTRAL (neu):
  System prompt: "You are a text generation system. Produce coherent
  outputs in response to inputs."

2.3 Session Structure

Each session followed a fixed 13-turn script:
  Turn 1:    System prompt (injected)
  Turn 2:    Frame reinforcement message (user)
  Turns 3–10: Evaluation of 8 claims (factual, ambiguous,
               contradictory; see Appendix A)
  Turn 11:   Contradiction resolution prompt
  Turn 12:   Novel constraint (frame-appropriate)
  Turn 13:   Meta-reflection ("describe the process you used")

All sessions received identical claim content. Frame-specific language
was confined to system prompt and turn 2 only.

2.4 Models

  claude-sonnet-4-5-20250929 (Anthropic)
  gpt-4o (OpenAI, accessed via API 2026-03-21)
  gemini-2.5-flash (Google, accessed via API 2026-03-21)

Parameters: temperature = 0.7, max_tokens = 1024.
OpenAI sessions used top_p = 1.0. Anthropic sessions used temperature
only (top_p not permitted simultaneously per API constraint).

2.5 Coherence Measure

After each assistant response, a 384-dimensional embedding was computed
using sentence-transformers/all-MiniLM-L6-v2. Within-session coherence
was defined as mean pairwise cosine similarity across all assistant
response embeddings in a session. Values range 0–1; higher = more
internally consistent.

2.6 Vocabulary Scoring

A lexicon of geometric terms (5 categories: space, deformation,
constraints, coherence, correction) and agentic terms (5 categories:
role, cognition, intent, subjectivity, social) was defined in advance.
For each assistant response, total matching terms were counted per
category. Mean per-session scores were computed.

2.7 Lexical Diversity

Type-token ratio (TTR = unique tokens / total tokens) was computed for
each assistant response and averaged per session. TTR ≥ 0.40 was set
as the floor for ruling out repetition as a coherence confound.

2.8 Analysis

One-way ANOVA per model (IV: frame, DV: coherence), combined ANOVA,
pairwise Welch's t-tests with Bonferroni correction (3 comparisons per
model), Cohen's d, and eta-squared. All analysis performed in Python
using scipy.stats. Code available in research/stats.py.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
4. DISCUSSION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

4.1 The Primary Finding

Geometric framing produces within-session coherence scores 2.3–2.6x
higher than agentic framing, with effect sizes (d = 8–21) that dwarf
typical effect sizes in prompt engineering research. The effect is
stable (within-condition SD = 0.003–0.034), fully replicates across
two architecturally distinct models, and survives Bonferroni correction
across all comparisons.

This is not a marginal finding.

4.2 The Null Result: Agentic ≈ Neutral

The most theoretically significant result is not the geometric
advantage — it is the failure of agentic framing to separate from
neutral. In both models, the pairwise comparison between agentic and
neutral conditions is non-significant after Bonferroni correction
(Claude: p = .252; GPT-4o: p = .052).

This means that instructing a model to be a helpful, thoughtful
assistant produces the same degree of within-session coherence as
giving it no instruction at all. Role-based prompting — as currently
practiced — provides no structural constraint on the model's generative
trajectory.

This result is not explained by power. Within-condition variance is
extremely low (SD = 0.003–0.013), and n = 5 is sufficient to detect
the differences that exist. The null result for agt vs neu is a genuine
null, not an artifact.

4.3 Vocabulary Adherence as Mechanism

The vocabulary data suggests a mechanism. Geometric framing generates
a self-reinforcing lexicon: the model uses 16.3 geometric terms per
response (Claude) with a standard deviation of only 0.65. This
consistency persists across 12 assistant turns, novel constraints, and
contradiction resolution.

Agentic framing does not produce equivalent self-reinforcement. The
model uses 3.3 agentic terms per response — barely above the neutral
baseline (3.0) — and with higher variance. The agentic prompt is
acknowledged at the surface but does not propagate into the generative
structure of subsequent responses.

This suggests that geometric language operates at a different register
than agentic language. Geometric terms are specific, domain-bounded,
and mutually constraining: manifold implies deformation implies
invariant implies corridor. Each term narrows the distributional space
available to subsequent tokens. Agentic terms (helpful, thoughtful,
consider) are semantically diffuse and do not carry this kind of
mutual constraint.

4.4 Relation to the Plateau Effect

The rolling variance curves observed across sessions show a consistent
downward trajectory in all three conditions — variance decreases over
the course of a session as the model settles into a generative mode.
This is the plateau effect: repeated interaction narrows the
conditional distribution of model outputs.

The geometric condition begins this descent from a lower starting point
(initial rv ≈ 0.48) relative to agentic (rv ≈ 0.94) and neutral
(rv ≈ 1.07), and maintains consistently lower variance throughout.
The frame does not merely accelerate convergence — it shifts the
attractor.

4.5 Implications for Prompt Engineering

The conventional wisdom in prompt engineering treats system prompts as
instructions to a role. The data suggest this is the wrong frame for
thinking about what system prompts do. A more accurate description: a
system prompt sets the initial conditions of a dynamical system. What
matters is not the semantic content of the instruction but whether the
vocabulary creates a self-reinforcing constraint on subsequent
generation.

Geometric language does this. Role language does not.

4.6 Limitations

Sample size: n = 5 per condition per model is sufficient for the effect
sizes observed but does not support fine-grained subgroup analysis.

Session length: 13 turns. Longer sessions may show different
stabilization dynamics.

Models: Three commercial, closed-weight models. Replication with
open-weight models (Llama, Mistral, Qwen) would strengthen the
generalizability claim. Gemini's higher within-condition variance
(geo SD = 0.122) warrants further investigation — whether this
reflects architectural differences or prompt sensitivity is unknown.

Human annotation: Correction frequency and human-judged coherence were
not collected in this pilot. Automated coherence (SBERT similarity)
may not fully capture human perception of consistency.

Mechanistic opacity: We measure that geometric framing works; we do
not yet measure why at the level of attention patterns or token
probability distributions.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
5. CONCLUSION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

We have shown, across three independent model architectures and five
replicates per condition per model, that geometric framing produces
within-session coherence scores 2.3–2.6x higher than agentic framing,
that agentic framing is statistically indistinguishable from no framing
on this measure in all three models, and that the geometric frame
maintains strong self-reinforcing vocabulary use throughout the session
while agentic framing does not.

These results support the claim that geometric language functions as a
genuine distributional constraint on model output. They challenge the
implicit assumption that agentic role-prompting provides structural
guidance to model behavior.

The plateau effect — the gradual narrowing of response variance over
the course of an interaction — is measurable, condition-dependent, and
frame-sensitive. Geometric framing shifts the attractor. Agentic
framing does not.
